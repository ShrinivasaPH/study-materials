01
Introduction

What is Computer Vision?

Computer Vision (CV) is the branch of AI that teaches computers to interpret images and video the way humans do — or even better.

Plain English

When you look at a photo of a dog, your brain instantly recognises the animal, where it is in the photo, whether it's running or sitting, and even what breed it might be. Computer Vision is a set of algorithms and neural networks that teach a computer to do the same thing — by analysing the numbers that make up that image.

🧩
Analogy — The Jigsaw Puzzle Expert

Imagine giving a jigsaw puzzle to someone who has seen millions of other puzzles. They don't need to think hard — they immediately recognise patterns: "this shade of blue is sky", "this jagged edge is a mountain", "this texture is grass". A CV model works the same way — trained on millions of images, it learns to pattern-match at superhuman speed.

The CV Task Family — From Simple to Complex

CV isn't just one thing — it's a family of related tasks. Think of them as a ladder of increasing difficulty:

🐕 Image Classification

Question: "What is in this image?"

The model looks at the whole image and gives a single label. Like a librarian who reads the cover of a book and says "this is a science fiction novel".

Example: Instagram's auto-tag feature, Google Photos album sorting, skin disease screening.

Simplest Task

📦 Object Detection

Question: "What is in this image AND where is it?"

Draws a bounding box around every object it finds. Like a security guard who spots the shoplifter AND points to exactly where they're standing.

Example: Self-driving car pedestrian detection, CCTV surveillance, autonomous drones.

Medium Difficulty

🎭 Semantic Segmentation

Question: "Which pixels belong to which object?"

Instead of a box, it paints each pixel with a colour-coded label. Like a professional painter who stays perfectly within the lines of each object.

Example: Medical image analysis (tumour boundaries), portrait mode background blur.

Hard Task

🧍 Pose Estimation

Question: "What is this person's body doing?"

Maps 17+ body keypoints (shoulder, elbow, knee…) to understand posture and movement. Like a coach watching an athlete's form in slow motion.

Example: Sports performance analysis, physical therapy apps, video game motion capture.

Specialised Task

Foundations

How Computers See Images

Before any AI magic happens, a computer must first answer: "What even is an image?" The answer is surprisingly simple — and profoundly powerful.

Plain English

You see a photo as a beautiful landscape. Your computer sees it as a massive spreadsheet of numbers. Each cell in the spreadsheet represents one tiny dot (pixel) of the image. For a colour image, each dot has three numbers — one for how Red it is, one for Green, and one for Blue. That's it. All the magic of Computer Vision is just mathematics applied to these numbers.

What Exactly Is a Pixel?

The word "pixel" comes from picture element — the smallest single point of colour in a digital image. If you've ever seen an extreme close-up of a phone screen, you've seen pixels — tiny coloured squares.

For a grayscale image (black & white), each pixel is just one number from 0 to 255. Zero means pure black, 255 means pure white, everything in between is a shade of grey.

For a colour (RGB) image, each pixel is three numbers stacked together: how much Red, how much Green, and how much Blue. Mix them in different proportions and you get millions of colours.

Image Shape = (Height, Width, Channels)
Example: a 1080p image = (1080, 1920, 3) = 6,220,800 individual numbers!
🤯

A standard 12-megapixel phone photo contains 36 million numbers. A CV model reads and processes all of them in under 50 milliseconds.

🖼️
Analogy — Mosaic Tiles

A mosaic art piece is made of thousands of tiny coloured tiles. From far away you see a beautiful portrait. Up close you just see squares. An image is exactly the same — zoom out enough and your eyes fill the gaps.

RGB isn't the only way to represent colour. Different tasks need different representations — just like how you might describe a paint colour as "forest green" to a friend, but as "#228B22" to a web developer.

Hue is the "pure" colour (0–360°), Saturation is how vivid it is, Value is how bright. Much more intuitive for selecting and isolating colours.

03
Preprocessing

Image Preprocessing

Before any model ever sees your data, the images must be transformed into a consistent, model-friendly format. Preprocessing alone can swing accuracy by 10–15 percentage points.

Plain English

Imagine teaching a child to recognise dogs using photos that are all different sizes, some blurry, some too dark, some upside-down. The inconsistency would confuse them. Neural networks have the same problem. Preprocessing standardises every image into the same "language" — same size, same numeric range, same colour scale — so the model wastes zero capacity learning about your camera and instead focuses entirely on learning about what's in the image.

1 — Resize & Aspect Ratio

Neural networks require fixed-size inputs. ResNet-50 always expects 224×224 px. Inception expects 299×299. Your phone shoots 4032×3024. You must bridge this gap — but how you resize matters.

Naive squashing distorts the image (faces become thin). The standard approach: resize the shorter side first, then crop the centre. This preserves aspect ratio while delivering the exact pixel count the model expects.

scale = target / min(H, W) → new_H = H×scale, new_W = W×scale
then CenterCrop to (224, 224)
Resize shorter side → crop. No distortion.
📐
Analogy — Passport Photo

A passport photo must be exactly 35×45 mm. The photographer zooms in on your face — not squash it. Resize to fit, then crop the relevant region.

The three resize strategies
Resize + CenterCrop validation
Resize shorter side to 256 → crop 224×224 from centre. Deterministic — gives the same crop every time. Used at test time.
RandomResizedCrop training
Random scale (8%–100% of image) + random aspect ratio → resize to 224. Forces the model to recognise objects at different scales. Huge impact on accuracy.
Letterboxing YOLO / detection
Resize to fit inside target box, pad remainder with grey (128,128,128). Zero distortion — critical for detection where exact box coordinates matter.

2 — Normalisation & Standardisation

Raw pixels are integers from 0 to 255. Neural networks train much faster when inputs are small, zero-centred floating-point numbers. There are two levels of normalisation, and most production pipelines use both.

Level 1 — Scale to [0, 1]

x_norm = x / 255.0
uint8 [0–255] → float32 [0.0–1.0]

Simply divides every pixel by 255. Now all values are in the same small range and the model sees the image consistently regardless of brightness scale. This is always the minimum you should do.

Level 2 — Standardise with Mean & Std

x_std = (x_norm − mean) / std
mean=[0.485,0.456,0.406] std=[0.229,0.224,0.225]

Shifts each channel to have mean≈0 and std≈1. The specific numbers above are computed from the entire 1.2 million image ImageNet dataset. If you use a pretrained ImageNet model, you must use these exact values — the model's weights were calibrated to this distribution.

💡

Why does it matter? Without normalisation, the gradient for the blue channel (small values) would be tiny while the red channel gradient (large values) would be huge — different learning speeds per channel. Normalisation puts all channels on equal footing so the optimizer can use a single stable learning rate.

3 — Data Augmentation

You rarely have enough labelled training data. Augmentation artificially multiplies your dataset by applying random transformations at training time — each epoch the model sees a slightly different version of every image. At test time, no augmentation is applied.

Geometric Transforms

Flip (horizontal only for most tasks — vertical flip of a car makes no sense), rotate (±15°), random crop, translate, shear. Teaches the model that objects can appear anywhere and at any angle.

RandomHorizontalFlip(p=0.5)
RandomRotation(degrees=15)
RandomAffine(shear=10)

Colour Jitter

Random changes to brightness, contrast, saturation, and hue. Teaches the model that a cat is still a cat whether it's in bright sunlight, indoor lighting, or shadow. Especially important for real-world deployment.

ColorJitter(
brightness=0.3,
contrast=0.3,
saturation=0.2,
hue=0.1)

Erasing & Mixing

Advanced techniques: RandomErasing cuts out random rectangles (forces model to not rely on any single region). CutMix pastes a patch from one image into another. MixUp blends two images at the pixel level.

RandomErasing(p=0.3)
CutMix(alpha=1.0)
MixUp(alpha=0.2)
When to augment — and when NOT to
✅ Augment during training
Every epoch, each image is transformed differently. Effectively multiplies your dataset by the number of epochs. The model sees 50 different crops of the same cat across 50 epochs.
❌ Never augment during validation/testing
Validation must be deterministic to measure progress reliably. Only resize + centre crop + normalise. Otherwise your accuracy numbers would change every run.

4 — Contrast Enhancement: Histogram Equalisation & CLAHE

Poorly lit images have pixels bunched into a narrow range — most values are dark (0–80) or most are bright (180–255). The model loses contrast information it needs. Histogram Equalisation spreads the intensity values across the full 0–255 range, boosting contrast automatically.

CLAHE (Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation) is a smarter version: it divides the image into small tiles and equalises each one separately, then stitches them back. This prevents over-boosting uniform regions while dramatically improving local detail in medical images, satellite images, and nighttime footage.

📊
Analogy — Volume Normalisation

Like a sound engineer who brings all tracks to the same loudness — the quiet instruments become audible without clipping the loud ones. CLAHE does this independently for every region of the image.

CLAHE in Python
# Convert to LAB — equalise only Lightness channel
img_lab = cv2.cvtColor(img, cv2.COLOR_BGR2LAB)
L, a, b = cv2.split(img_lab)

# CLAHE: clipLimit prevents over-amplification
# tileGridSize controls local region size
clahe  = cv2.createCLAHE(clipLimit=2.0,
                          tileGridSize=(8, 8))
L_eq   = clahe.apply(L)

# Merge back and convert to BGR
img_eq = cv2.cvtColor(
    cv2.merge([L_eq, a, b]),
    cv2.COLOR_LAB2BGR)

# Simple (global) histogram equalisation
gray_eq = cv2.equalizeHist(gray)

5 — Noise Removal

Real-world images contain noise — random pixel variations from sensor heat, low light, or JPEG compression artifacts. Noise looks like edges to a convolution filter, and can flood your feature maps with garbage. Removing noise before edge detection is not optional — it's step one of every classical pipeline.

G
Gaussian Blur — most common

Replaces each pixel with a weighted average of neighbours. Sigma σ controls blur strength. Used before Canny, Sobel. σ=1–2 for mild denoising.

M
Median Filter — for salt & pepper noise

Replaces each pixel with the median (not mean) of its neighbourhood. Outlier pixels (extreme white/black dots) are removed without blurring edges — better than Gaussian for impulsive noise.

B
Bilateral Filter — edge-preserving blur

Like Gaussian blur, but it only averages pixels that are both spatially close AND have similar intensity. Result: smooth regions get blurred, but edges stay sharp. Used in medical imaging and portrait photo processing.

N
Non-Local Means (NLM) — highest quality

Searches the entire image for patches similar to the current patch and averages them. Removes even heavy noise while preserving fine textures. Slow — best for offline processing.

Denoising options in OpenCV
# 1. Gaussian — fast, mild
blurred = cv2.GaussianBlur(img, (5,5), sigmaX=1.4)

# 2. Median — removes salt & pepper
median  = cv2.medianBlur(img, ksize=5)

# 3. Bilateral — preserves edges
bilat   = cv2.bilateralFilter(
    img,
    d=9,          # neighbourhood diameter
    sigmaColor=75, # intensity similarity
    sigmaSpace=75  # spatial closeness
)

# 4. Non-Local Means — highest quality
nlm = cv2.fastNlMeansDenoisingColored(
    img,
    h=10,          # filter strength
    hColor=10,
    templateWindowSize=7,
    searchWindowSize=21
)

6 — Thresholding: Separating Object from Background

Thresholding converts a grayscale image into a binary (black/white) image by labelling every pixel as either object or background based on its intensity. It's one of the oldest and most useful operations in classical CV — used in document scanning, cell counting, industrial inspection, and as a pre-step for contour detection.

G
Global Threshold

Pick one value T. Every pixel above T → white (255); below T → black (0). Works only when lighting is uniform across the whole image.

out(x,y) = 255 if I(x,y) > T, else 0
O
Otsu's Method — Auto Global Threshold

Otsu's algorithm automatically finds the optimal T by minimising intra-class pixel variance. Works perfectly for bimodal histograms (clear foreground + background peak). You pass the image and OpenCV computes T for you.

A
Adaptive Threshold — For Uneven Lighting

Instead of one global T, the image is divided into small regions and a separate threshold is computed for each. Essential for shadows, spotlights, or scanned handwritten documents where brightness varies across the image.

Thresholding in Python (OpenCV)
# ── 1. Simple global threshold (T=127) ──────────
_, thresh_global = cv2.threshold(
    gray, 127, 255, cv2.THRESH_BINARY)

# ── 2. Otsu — auto-computed optimal T ───────────
# Returns (computed_T, binary_image)
T, thresh_otsu = cv2.threshold(
    gray, 0, 255,
    cv2.THRESH_BINARY + cv2.THRESH_OTSU)
print(f"Otsu found T = {T:.0f}")

# ── 3. Adaptive — local threshold per region ────
thresh_adapt = cv2.adaptiveThreshold(
    gray,
    maxValue=255,
    adaptiveMethod=cv2.ADAPTIVE_THRESH_GAUSSIAN_C,
    thresholdType=cv2.THRESH_BINARY,
    blockSize=11,    # size of local neighbourhood
    C=2)             # constant subtracted from mean

# ── Inverse: white background → black objects ───
_, thresh_inv = cv2.threshold(
    gray, 0, 255,
    cv2.THRESH_BINARY_INV + cv2.THRESH_OTSU)
🔦

Global threshold = one light switch for the whole room. Adaptive threshold = individual dimmers for each corner.

7 — Morphological Operations: Shaping Binary Images

After thresholding you often get noisy results — tiny holes inside objects, stray pixels, or objects that are almost-but-not-quite touching. Morphological operations use a small structuring element (a kernel) to sculpt binary masks into the desired shape.

Erosion — Shrink & Remove Noise

A pixel is kept white only if all pixels under the kernel are white. Shrinks objects and removes thin protrusions or isolated white dots (salt noise). The border of each white region gets eaten away.

Erosion(A, B) = { z | Bz ⊆ A }
Keep pixel only if full kernel fits inside object A

Dilation — Grow & Fill Gaps

A pixel is set white if any pixel under the kernel is white. Expands objects and fills small holes or gaps. The border of each white region grows outward.

Dilation(A, B) = { z | Bz ∩ A ≠ ∅ }
Set pixel if any overlap between kernel and object A

Opening = Erosion then Dilation

Removes small objects and thin connections while preserving the size of large objects. Use it to clean up noise after thresholding. A noisy binary mask → opening → clean object.

Noisy mask→Opening→Clean object

Closing = Dilation then Erosion

Fills small holes inside objects and closes small gaps between objects. Use it when your thresholded object has tiny holes or broken contours. Preserves overall object size.

Holey mask→Closing→Filled object
Morphological Operations in Python
# Structuring element (kernel shape)
kernel = cv2.getStructuringElement(
    cv2.MORPH_RECT,   # rectangle kernel
    (5, 5))           # MORPH_ELLIPSE / MORPH_CROSS

# Erosion — shrink, remove noise
eroded = cv2.erode(binary, kernel, iterations=1)

# Dilation — expand, fill gaps
dilated = cv2.dilate(binary, kernel, iterations=1)
# Opening: erode then dilate (remove noise)
opened = cv2.morphologyEx(binary,
    cv2.MORPH_OPEN, kernel)

# Closing: dilate then erode (fill holes)
closed = cv2.morphologyEx(binary,
    cv2.MORPH_CLOSE, kernel)

# Morphological gradient: dilation − erosion
# Gives you bright object outlines
gradient = cv2.morphologyEx(binary,
    cv2.MORPH_GRADIENT, kernel)
💡

Practical recipe for clean segmentation: threshold (Otsu) → morphological opening (remove noise) → morphological closing (fill holes) → find contours. These four lines solve 80% of classical segmentation problems.

Image Processing

Filters & Convolution

Before deep learning can analyse an image, we apply classical mathematical operations to clean, sharpen, and highlight the features that matter most.

Plain English

Imagine sliding a small, transparent magnifying lens across a photo. At each position, the lens highlights certain things — maybe just the edges, or just the blurry regions. In image processing, this "lens" is called a kernel or filter, and the act of sliding it across the image is called convolution.

Convolution — The Core Operation

A kernel is a tiny grid of numbers (usually 3×3 or 5×5). We slide it over every position in the image. At each position we multiply the kernel values with the underlying pixel values and sum them up — this gives one output number. The collection of all these output numbers forms a feature map.

(I ⊛ K)[i,j] = Σₘ Σₙ I[i+m, j+n] · K[m,n]
I = Input Image · K = Kernel · [i,j] = output position · ⊛ = convolution
🍳
Analogy — Cooking with a Stencil

Think of pressing a cookie-cutter shape into dough. At each position it leaves the same pattern, but the flavour of the dough (the pixel values) changes the result. Different kernel shapes emphasise different image features — edge kernels reveal outlines, blur kernels average neighbours, sharpen kernels boost fine detail.

Stride, Padding & Pooling — Controlling the Output Size

Stride — How Far We Jump

Stride controls how many pixels the kernel jumps each step. Stride-1 means slide one pixel at a time (fine detail). Stride-2 means jump two pixels (halves the output, faster).

STRIDE = 1 (9 outputs)
STRIDE = 2 (4 outputs)

Like scanning a photo with a scanner — slow (stride-1) gives maximum detail; fast (stride-2) gives a smaller, coarser result.

Pooling — Summarising a Region

Pooling reduces the spatial size of the feature map by summarising a region of pixels with a single value. It adds translation invariance — small shifts in the object's position don't change the output much.

Deep Learning

Convolutional Neural Networks

CNNs are the engines that power almost all of modern Computer Vision. They automatically learn what to look for — no hand-coding required.

Plain English

A CNN is like a multi-stage factory inspection line. The first workers look for very simple things (is there a horizontal line here?). Their findings get passed to the next workers, who look for slightly more complex things (is there a corner shape?). Further down the line, workers look for even more complex things (is there a nose? an eye?). By the final stage, a supervisor has all the reports and says "Yep — that's a face." The key insight: the workers learn what to look for by themselves, just by seeing millions of labelled examples.

The Single Neuron — Building Block of Everything

A neuron takes a list of numbers as input, multiplies each by a weight, adds them all up (plus a bias), and passes the result through an activation function. That's the entire operation.

y = f(w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + ... + wₙxₙ + b)
wᵢ = learned weights · xᵢ = inputs · b = bias · f = activation

The weights start random. Through training, they adjust to recognise meaningful patterns. A single neuron can answer yes/no questions like "is there a horizontal edge here?" — a layer of 64 neurons answers 64 such questions simultaneously.

Forward Pass vs. Backward Pass

Training a CNN alternates two operations millions of times:

→
Forward Pass

Feed the image through all layers in order. Each layer transforms the data. The final layer outputs a prediction (e.g., "90% cat, 8% dog, 2% bird").

L
Compute Loss

Compare the prediction to the true label using a loss function (e.g., Cross-Entropy). The loss is a single number measuring "how wrong was this prediction?"

←
Backward Pass (Backpropagation)

Using calculus (chain rule), compute how much each weight contributed to the error. Then nudge every weight slightly in the direction that reduces the loss — by a step size called the learning rate.

🎯 Loss Functions

Cross-Entropy Loss — standard for classification. Penalises wrong confident predictions very heavily. Pushes the model to assign high probability to the correct class.

L = −Σ yᵢ · log(ŷᵢ)
yᵢ = true label ŷᵢ = predicted prob

Other losses: MSE (regression), Focal Loss (class imbalance), BCE (binary), Dice (segmentation).

⚡ Optimizers

SGD — classic stochastic gradient descent. Simple: w = w − lr × ∂L/∂w. Needs careful learning rate tuning and momentum.

Adam / AdamW — adaptive per-parameter learning rates. Each weight has its own step size that self-adjusts. Usually converges faster. AdamW adds proper weight decay (L2 reg).

Rule of thumb: Start with AdamW lr=1e-3. Use SGD + momentum for squeezing the last 1%.

📉 Learning Rate Schedules

The learning rate controls how big each update step is. Too large → training diverges. Too small → training is glacially slow.

Cosine Annealing: starts high, smoothly decreases to near zero following a cosine curve. The warm restarts variant can escape local minima.

Warmup: start with a tiny lr for the first few epochs (the model is initialised randomly — large updates are destructive). Ramp up, then anneal.

BatchNorm & Dropout — The Stabilisers

📦 Batch Normalisation

A layer's inputs keep shifting as the weights in previous layers update — the model is chasing a moving target. BatchNorm re-normalises the output of each layer back to mean≈0, std≈1 after every mini-batch, then applies learned scale (γ) and shift (β) parameters.

BN(x) = γ · (x − μ_batch) / √(σ²_batch + ε) + β
✓10× faster training (less careful lr tuning needed)
✓Acts as a regulariser — reduces overfitting
✓Allows much higher learning rates
⚠️During inference, uses running mean/var (not batch stats)

💧 Dropout

During each training forward pass, Dropout randomly sets p% of neuron outputs to zero. Different neurons get zeroed every pass — the network can never rely on any single neuron. This forces it to learn redundant representations, which generalise much better to unseen data.

mask ~ Bernoulli(1 − p) → output = x × mask / (1 − p)
p=0.5 means 50% of neurons zeroed. Divide by (1−p) to keep expected value the same.
✓Powerful regulariser — prevents co-adaptation of neurons
⚠️Must be disabled at inference time (model.eval() in PyTorch)
→Dropout2d zeroes entire feature maps, not individual pixels

Receptive Field — How Much of the Image Does a Neuron "See"?

Each neuron in a conv layer only looks at a small patch of the previous layer. But after stacking multiple conv layers, a neuron in a deep layer has an effective receptive field that spans a much larger region of the original image — it's been informed by contributions from a large neighbourhood.

A 3×3 conv applied twice gives a 5×5 effective receptive field on the original image. Applied three times: 7×7. This grows with every layer — which is exactly why deep networks can recognise large, complex objects: their deep neurons can "see" the entire object at once.

RF_L = RF_(L-1) + (kernel_size − 1) × stride_product
For stride=1, k=3: RF grows by 2 with each layer.
After 5 layers: RF = 1 + 5×2 = 11×11

🎲 Weight Initialisation

Weights can't start at zero — every neuron would learn the same thing (symmetry breaking problem). They can't start too large — activations explode. They can't start too small — gradients vanish. The solution: initialise with small random values scaled to the layer size.

Xavier: W ~ U(−√(6/(fan_in+fan_out)), +√(6/(fan_in+fan_out)))
Designed for Tanh/Sigmoid. Keeps variance constant across layers.
He: W ~ N(0, √(2/fan_in))
Designed for ReLU. Accounts for the half-zeroing of ReLU. Default for modern CNNs.

PyTorch applies He init by default for Conv2d and Linear layers. You rarely need to set this manually unless you use custom activations.

⚖️ L1 & L2 Regularisation

Adding a penalty term to the loss that discourages large weights. Large weights create brittle, over-confident models that memorise training data instead of learning general patterns.

L2: Loss_total = Loss + λ · Σ wᵢ²
Penalises squared weights. Pushes all weights towards zero but not exactly zero. Default in AdamW via weight_decay=1e-4.
L1: Loss_total = Loss + λ · Σ |wᵢ|
Penalises absolute weights. Drives unimportant weights to exactly zero (sparse model). Used in Lasso regression and sparse feature selection.

Plain English

A student who memorises past exam papers can answer those exact questions perfectly but fails the real exam. That's overfitting — perfect on training data, poor on new data. Activation Functions — The Non-Linear Magic

Without activation functions, no matter how many layers you stack, a neural network is just doing linear algebra — equivalent to one layer. Activations introduce non-linearity, letting the network learn curves, edges, and complex shapes.

ReLU — Rectified Linear Unit

f(x) = max(0, x)

If the input is negative, output 0. Otherwise pass it through. Dead simple — and that's the point. Fast, efficient, rarely saturates.

🚦

Like a one-way valve. Negative signals are blocked; positive signals pass through unchanged.

Sigmoid

σ(x) = 1 / (1 + e⁻ˣ)

Squashes any input to a value between 0 and 1. Perfect for binary outputs (yes/no, spam/ham). Can cause vanishing gradients in deep nets.

🎚️

Like a dimmer switch — extreme inputs (very on or very off) get squeezed towards the middle.

P(yᵢ) = eˣⁱ / Σⱼ eˣʲ
Logits: [3.2, 1.1, 0.2]

Object Detection

Detection answers two questions simultaneously: what is in the image, and where exactly is it? This dual challenge makes it far harder than classification.

Plain English

Imagine you're a security guard watching a CCTV feed. You don't just need to say "there's a person in this building" — you need to point to them and say "person at X,Y with 94% confidence". That's object detection: classification + location, both at the same time, for every object in the scene.

Intersection over Union (IoU)

How do we measure whether a predicted bounding box is good? We use IoU — the ratio of the overlap area to the total combined area of the two boxes.

IoU = Area(Pred ∩ GT) / Area(Pred ∪ GT)
GT = Ground Truth box · Range: 0.0 (no overlap) → 1.0 (perfect)
IoU < 0.3

Poor — boxes barely overlap. Detection effectively missed.

IoU = 0.5

The traditional "good enough" threshold (PASCAL VOC standard).

IoU > 0.9

Excellent — nearly perfect localisation of the object.

Non-Maximum Suppression — The Clean-Up Step

Every detection model generates many overlapping boxes for the same object (the model is uncertain about the exact location). NMS is the post-processing step that keeps only the best one.

Plain English

Imagine 5 people each pointing at the same cat from slightly different angles. NMS finds which person is most confident, keeps their answer, and tells the others to sit down — but only if they were all pointing to the same cat (overlap > threshold).

1
Sort by confidence

Rank all predicted boxes from highest to lowest confidence score.

2
Take the top box

The highest-confidence box is kept and added to final results.

3
Suppress overlapping boxes

Any remaining box with IoU > 0.5 against the kept box is deleted.

4
Repeat

Go back to step 2 until no boxes remain. Each iteration keeps the best un-suppressed box.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

import requests

# ── Step 1: Gaussian Blur ─────────────────────────────────
# Before finding edges we MUST reduce noise.
# Without this step, noise looks like thousands of tiny edges.
# ksize=(5,5) = kernel size.  sigmaX=1.4 = how spread the blur is.
blurred = cv2.GaussianBlur(gray, (5, 5), sigmaX=1.4)

# ── Step 2: Sobel Gradients ───────────────────────────────
# Sobel-X detects vertical edges (left-right brightness change)
# Sobel-Y detects horizontal edges (top-bottom brightness change)
# CV_64F lets us keep negative values (edges in both directions)
sobel_x = cv2.Sobel(blurred, cv2.CV_64F, 1, 0, ksize=3)
sobel_y = cv2.Sobel(blurred, cv2.CV_64F, 0, 1, ksize=3)

# Combine: edge strength = sqrt(Gx² + Gy²)
magnitude = np.sqrt(sobel_x**2 + sobel_y**2)
magnitude = np.uint8(magnitude / magnitude.max() * 255)

# ── Step 3: Canny Edge Detector ───────────────────────────
# NOTE: cv2.Canny takes positional thresholds — NOT keyword args
# threshold1=50:  below this = not an edge (discarded)
# threshold2=150: above this = definitely an edge (kept)
# Values between are kept only if connected to a strong edge (hysteresis)
# Good ratio: 1:2 or 1:3  (low:high)
edges = cv2.Canny(blurred, 50, 150)

# ── Step 4: Custom Kernel via filter2D ───────────────────
# You can apply ANY kernel manually using filter2D
# This is the Laplacian kernel — sharpens / detects blobs
laplacian_kernel = np.array([[-1,-1,-1],
                               [-1, 8,-1],
                               [-1,-1,-1]], dtype=np.float32)
custom = cv2.filter2D(gray, -1, laplacian_kernel)

# ── Visualise the pipeline ────────────────────────────────
titles = ['Original', 'Gaussian Blur', 'Sobel X', 'Sobel Y', 'Gradient Magnitude', 'Canny Edges']
images = [gray, blurred,
          np.clip(np.abs(sobel_x), 0, 255).astype('uint8'),   # clip prevents overflow wrap
          np.clip(np.abs(sobel_y), 0, 255).astype('uint8'),   # float64 values can exceed 255
          magnitude, edges]

# ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# LAB 3: Train a CNN from scratch on CIFAR-10
# Classes: plane car bird cat deer dog frog horse ship truck
# pip install torch torchvision
# ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.optim as optim
import torchvision, torchvision.transforms as T

device = 'cuda' if torch.cuda.is_available() else 'cpu'
print(f"Training on: {device}")

# ── Transforms ────────────────────────────────────────────
# Training: random augmentations to prevent overfitting
# Validation: only normalise — no random transforms (deterministic)
CIFAR_MEAN = [0.4914, 0.4822, 0.4465]   # computed from CIFAR-10 training set
CIFAR_STD  = [0.2023, 0.1994, 0.2010]

transform_train = T.Compose([
    T.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
    T.RandomCrop(32, padding=4),              # pad 4 px each side, crop 32×32
    T.ColorJitter(brightness=0.2, contrast=0.2, saturation=0.1),
    T.ToTensor(),
    T.Normalize(CIFAR_MEAN, CIFAR_STD)
])
transform_val = T.Compose([
    T.ToTensor(),
    T.Normalize(CIFAR_MEAN, CIFAR_STD)
])

trainset    = torchvision.datasets.CIFAR10(root='./data', train=True,  download=True, transform=transform_train)
valset      = torchvision.datasets.CIFAR10(root='./data', train=False, download=True, transform=transform_val)
trainloader = torch.utils.data.DataLoader(trainset, batch_size=128, shuffle=True,  num_workers=0, pin_memory=True)
valloader   = torch.utils.data.DataLoader(valset,   batch_size=256, shuffle=False, num_workers=0)

# ── CNN Architecture ──────────────────────────────────────
# Conv2d(in, out, k): learns `out` filters each of size k×k
# BatchNorm2d: normalises channel activations → stable training
# MaxPool2d(2): halves spatial resolution (stride=2 by default)
# Dropout: use nn.Dropout (not Dropout2d) after Flatten for FC layers
class ConvNet(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self):
        super().__init__()
        self.features = nn.Sequential(
            # Block 1: 3→64 ch  | 32×32 → 16×16
            nn.Conv2d(3,  64, kernel_size=3, padding=1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(64), nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.Conv2d(64, 64, kernel_size=3, padding=1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(64), nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.MaxPool2d(2),                           # 32→16

            # Block 2: 64→128 ch | 16×16 → 8×8
            nn.Conv2d(64,  128, kernel_size=3, padding=1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(128), nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.Conv2d(128, 128, kernel_size=3, padding=1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(128), nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.MaxPool2d(2),                           # 16→8

            # Block 3: 128→256 ch | 8×8 → 4×4
            nn.Conv2d(128, 256, kernel_size=3, padding=1, bias=False),
            nn.BatchNorm2d(256), nn.ReLU(inplace=True),
            nn.MaxPool2d(2),                           # 8→4
        )
        self.classifier = nn.Sequential(
            nn.AdaptiveAvgPool2d((1, 1)),            # 4×4→1×1: robust to different input sizes
            nn.Flatten(),                              # 256×1×1 → 256
            nn.Linear(256, 512), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(0.5),                          # disable at eval() automatically
            nn.Linear(512, 10)                        # 10 CIFAR classes
        )
    def forward(self, x):
        return self.classifier(self.features(x))

model = ConvNet().to(device)
total = sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters())
print(f"Parameters: {total:,}")   # ~1.3M — small enough to train in minutes

# ── Loss, Optimizer, Scheduler ────────────────────────────
# label_smoothing=0.05: slightly softens one-hot targets — reduces overconfidence
# AdamW: Adam + proper L2 weight decay (not fused into gradient)
# CosineAnnealingLR: lr starts at 1e-3, smoothly decays to 1e-5 over 50 epochs
criterion = nn.CrossEntropyLoss(label_smoothing=0.05)
optimizer = optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=1e-3, weight_decay=1e-4)
scheduler = optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=50, eta_min=1e-5)

# ── Training Loop ─────────────────────────────────────────
best_val = 0.0
for epoch in range(50):
    # ── Train ──
    model.train()
    train_loss = train_correct = 0
    for imgs, labels in trainloader:
        imgs, labels = imgs.to(device), labels.to(device)
        optimizer.zero_grad()
        out  = model(imgs)
        loss = criterion(out, labels)
        loss.backward()
        optimizer.step()
        train_loss    += loss.item()
        train_correct += (out.argmax(1) == labels).sum().item()

    # ── Validate ──
    model.eval()
    val_correct = 0
    with torch.no_grad():
        for imgs, labels in valloader:
            out = model(imgs.to(device))
            val_correct += (out.argmax(1) == labels.to(device)).sum().item()

    train_acc = train_correct / len(trainset)
    val_acc   = val_correct   / len(valset)
    scheduler.step()

    if val_acc > best_val:
        best_val = val_acc
        torch.save(model.state_dict(), 'best_cifar.pth')

    if (epoch + 1) % 5 == 0:
        lr = optimizer.param_groups[0]['lr']
        print(f"Epoch {epoch+1:2d}/50 | train={train_acc:.3f} | val={val_acc:.3f} | best={best_val:.3f} | lr={lr:.1e}")

print(f"\n✅ Best val acc: {best_val*100:.1f}%  (human baseline: ~94%)")

# Strategy: freeze backbone → train head → fine-tune last block

#
# Expected folder layout:
#   data/train/class_a/img1.jpg ...

import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.optim as optim
from torchvision import models, transforms, datasets
from torchvision.models import ResNet50_Weights       # explicit import avoids deprecation
from torch.utils.data import DataLoader
import os

device      = 'cuda' if torch.cuda.is_available() else 'cpu'
NUM_CLASSES = 5   # ← change to your number of classes
print(f"Device: {device}")

# ── Load pretrained ResNet50 ──────────────────────────────
# ResNet50_Weights.IMAGENET1K_V2 = best available weights (torchvision ≥0.13)
# The model has learned rich visual features from 1.28M images in 1000 classes.
backbone = models.resnet50(weights=ResNet50_Weights.IMAGENET1K_V2)

# ── Freeze all backbone weights ──────────────────────────
# requires_grad = False: these params are skipped by the optimizer
# Only the new FC head will have gradients in Phase 1
for param in backbone.parameters():
    param.requires_grad = False

# ── Replace the classification head ──────────────────────
# backbone.fc is the last Linear(2048 → 1000) layer
# We replace it with Linear(2048 → NUM_CLASSES)
in_feats = backbone.fc.in_features                 # 2048 for ResNet50
backbone.fc = nn.Sequential(
    nn.Dropout(0.4),
    nn.Linear(in_feats, 512),
    nn.ReLU(),
    nn.Dropout(0.3),
    nn.Linear(512, NUM_CLASSES)
)
model = backbone.to(device)

trainable = sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters() if p.requires_grad)
total     = sum(p.numel() for p in model.parameters())
print(f"Trainable: {trainable:,} / {total:,} ({trainable/total*100:.1f}%)")

# ── Transforms ────────────────────────────────────────────
# MUST use ImageNet mean/std — the backbone expects this distribution
imagenet_norm = transforms.Normalize(
    mean=[0.485, 0.456, 0.406],
    std= [0.229, 0.224, 0.225])

tfm_train = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.RandomResizedCrop(224, scale=(0.7, 1.0)),
    transforms.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
    transforms.ColorJitter(brightness=0.3, contrast=0.3, saturation=0.2),
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    imagenet_norm
])
tfm_val = transforms.Compose([
    transforms.Resize(256),
    transforms.CenterCrop(224),
    transforms.ToTensor(),
    imagenet_norm
])

# ── Load data ─────────────────────────────────────────────
# ImageFolder expects: root/class_name/image.jpg
# It automatically assigns integer labels from sorted folder names.
train_root = 'data/train'
val_root   = 'data/val'

if not os.path.exists(train_root):
    print("⚠ No data/train folder found. Create it with class subfolders.")
    print("  Example: data/train/cats/cat001.jpg, data/train/dogs/dog001.jpg")
else:
    train_ds = datasets.ImageFolder(train_root, tfm_train)
    val_ds   = datasets.ImageFolder(val_root,   tfm_val)
    train_dl = DataLoader(train_ds, batch_size=32, shuffle=True,  num_workers=2)
    val_dl   = DataLoader(val_ds,   batch_size=64, shuffle=False, num_workers=2)
    print(f"Train: {len(train_ds)} images | Val: {len(val_ds)} images")
    print(f"Classes: {train_ds.classes}")

    criterion = nn.CrossEntropyLoss()
    best_acc  = 0.0

    # ── Two-phase training ─────────────────────────────────
    # Phase 1: only train the new head (backbone frozen)
    #          → fast convergence, safe — can't destroy ImageNet features
    # Phase 2: unfreeze layer4 (last ResNet block) + train end-to-end
    #          → adapts high-level features to your domain
    for phase, n_epochs in [('head-only', 5), ('fine-tune', 15)]:
        if phase == 'fine-tune':
            for param in model.layer4.parameters():   # unfreeze last ResNet block
                param.requires_grad = True
            optimizer = optim.AdamW([
                {'params': model.layer4.parameters(), 'lr': 1e-5},  # tiny lr for backbone
                {'params': model.fc.parameters(),     'lr': 1e-4},  # larger lr for head
            ])
        else:
            optimizer = optim.AdamW(model.fc.parameters(), lr=3e-4)

        scheduler = optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(optimizer, T_max=n_epochs)
        print(f"\n── Phase: {phase} ({n_epochs} epochs) ──")

        for epoch in range(n_epochs):
            model.train()
            correct = 0
            for imgs, labels in train_dl:
                imgs, labels = imgs.to(device), labels.to(device)
                optimizer.zero_grad()
                out  = model(imgs)
                loss = criterion(out, labels)
                loss.backward()
                optimizer.step()
                correct += (out.argmax(1) == labels).sum().item()
            train_acc = correct / len(train_ds)

            model.eval()
            val_c = 0
            with torch.no_grad():
                for imgs, labels in val_dl:
                    out = model(imgs.to(device))
                    val_c += (out.argmax(1) == labels.to(device)).sum().item()
            val_acc = val_c / len(val_ds)

            if val_acc > best_acc:
                best_acc = val_acc
                torch.save(model.state_dict(), 'best_transfer.pth')

            scheduler.step()
            print(f"  Epoch {epoch+1:2d}/{n_epochs}  train={train_acc:.3f}  val={val_acc:.3f}  best={best_acc:.3f}")

    print(f"\n✅ Best val accuracy: {best_acc*100:.1f}%")
    print("Typical result: 90–97% with just 100–300 images per class!")

Training a model is only half the work. You need to measure how good it actually is — and the right metric depends entirely on what your model is used for.

Plain English

Imagine testing a spam filter. "Accuracy" sounds great — but if 99% of your emails are not spam, a filter that labels everything as "not spam" would be 99% accurate, while being completely useless. That's why we need metrics that are honest about the specific kinds of errors that matter.

Analogy — The Fire Alarm

A fire alarm that never fires has zero false alarms (no FP) — but it will miss every real fire (all FN). One that fires constantly never misses a fire (no FN) — but everyone ignores it due to false alarms (all FP). The art is in the balance.

The Key Metrics — Defined Simply

Precision — "When I say YES, am I right?"

Precision = TP / (TP + FP)

Of all the times the model raised an alert, what fraction were real? High precision = few false alarms. Important when false positives are costly (e.g., wrongly flagging a healthy person as sick).

Recall — "Of all real cases, how many did I catch?"

Recall = TP / (TP + FN)

Of all the real positives, how many did the model find? High recall = few misses. Critical when missing a case is dangerous (e.g., failing to detect cancer, missing a pedestrian).

F1 Score — The Balance

F1 = 2 × (Precision × Recall) / (Precision + Recall)

The harmonic mean of Precision and Recall. A single number that captures both. Useful when you want neither too many false alarms nor too many misses.
