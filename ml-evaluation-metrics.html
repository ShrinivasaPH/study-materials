Evaluation Metrics
Demystified

A hands-on guide to understanding, computing, and choosing the right metrics for every ML problem — with interactive tools and plain-English intuition.

Section 01

Classification Metrics

When your model predicts a category — spam vs. not spam, cat vs. dog, disease vs. healthy — you need these metrics to measure how well it performs.

💡
Before diving in, remember: there is no single "best" metric. The right one depends entirely on what mistakes cost more in your specific problem. Missing a cancer diagnosis? Precision and Recall matter most. Ranking search results? NDCG. Balanced classes with equal error costs? Accuracy is fine.
🎯
Accuracy
The most intuitive metric

The fraction of all predictions your model got right — both positive and negative classes combined.

Formula
Accuracy = (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)
Plain English
Out of 100 predictions, how many were correct? If 87 were right, accuracy = 87%.
Imagine a weather app that predicts "sunny" every day in Phoenix, Arizona. Since Phoenix is sunny ~300 days a year, the app gets ~82% accuracy — without any real intelligence! This is why accuracy fails badly with imbalanced classes. A fraud detection model that labels everything "not fraud" can hit 99.9% accuracy while being completely useless.
Simple to understand Fails on imbalanced data Balanced datasets
🔬
Precision
Of what you caught, what's real?

When the model predicted "positive", how often was it actually correct? Measures the quality of positive predictions.

Formula
Precision = TP / (TP + FP)
Plain English
Of all the emails your spam filter flagged, what percentage were actually spam? A low precision means lots of innocent emails landing in spam (false alarms).
Prioritize precision when false positives are costly:

• Spam filter: Flagging important business email as spam is terrible UX
• Court AI: Convicting an innocent person is far worse than letting one guilty person go
• Drug recommendation: Recommending a wrong drug causes harm
• Ad targeting: Showing irrelevant ads wastes budget
Spam detection False alarm costs May miss true positives
🧲
Recall (Sensitivity)
Of all real positives, what did you catch?

Out of all the actual positives in the dataset, how many did the model correctly identify? Also called True Positive Rate or Sensitivity.

Formula
Recall = TP / (TP + FN)
Plain English
Imagine 100 real cancer patients. If your model catches 90 of them, recall = 90%. The 10 it missed are dangerous false negatives.
Prioritize recall when missing a positive is catastrophic:

• Cancer screening: Missing a tumor is far worse than a false alarm
• Fraud detection: Letting fraud pass is costly (though some precision needed too)
• Airport security: Missing a real threat is unacceptable
• Product recalls: Missing a defective product causes safety risks
Medical diagnosis Safety-critical systems May cause false alarms
⚖️
F1-Score
The harmonic mean of P & R

A single metric that balances both Precision and Recall. Preferred when class distribution is uneven and both types of errors matter.

Formula
F1 = 2 × (P × R) / (P + R)
Plain English
F1 is the "average" of Precision and Recall — but uses the harmonic mean, which punishes extreme imbalance. A model with P=100%, R=0% gets F1=0 (useless), not 50%.
Why not just average P and R? Because (100% + 0%) / 2 = 50%, which looks great but the model is useless. The harmonic mean forces both values to be high for a good F1 score.

F-beta score generalizes this: F-beta = (1+β²) × (P×R) / (β²×P + R). Setting β=2 weights Recall twice as much (medical use). β=0.5 weights Precision more (legal use).
Imbalanced datasets NLP tasks Balances P & R
🛡️
Specificity
True Negative Rate

Of all the actual negative cases, how many did the model correctly identify as negative? The "negative recall" — often used alongside Recall in medical tests.

Formula
Specificity = TN / (TN + FP)
Plain English
100 healthy people take a COVID test. Your model correctly identifies 95 as healthy. Specificity = 95%. The 5 false alarms sent healthy people into unnecessary quarantine.
Recall (sensitivity) and Specificity are in a natural tension. A model that predicts "positive" for everyone gets 100% Recall but 0% Specificity. This trade-off is exactly what the ROC curve visualizes. Good diagnostic tests try to maximize both — but the right balance depends on the cost of each type of error.
Medical screening ROC curve companion
📐
MCC
Matthews Correlation Coefficient

Considered one of the most reliable single classification metrics — especially for imbalanced datasets. Returns a value between -1 and +1. +1 = perfect, 0 = random, -1 = inverse.

Formula
MCC = (TP×TN − FP×FN) / √((TP+FP)(TP+FN)(TN+FP)(TN+FN))
Plain English
MCC is like a correlation coefficient between your predictions and ground truth. Unlike F1, it considers all four cells of the confusion matrix — so it can't be "gamed" by predicting only one class.
Consider 1000 samples: 990 negative, 10 positive. A model that always predicts "negative" gets:
• Accuracy: 99% ✓ (misleading!)
• F1: 0 ✗ (ignores TN)
• MCC: 0 ✗ (correctly shows random)

MCC treats TP, TN, FP, FN equally and symmetrically. It's the metric recommended by many ML researchers when classes are severely imbalanced.
Severe imbalance Biomedical -1 to +1 range

Section 03

ROC Curve & AUC

The ROC curve shows model performance across ALL possible thresholds. AUC summarizes it in one number — a model's ability to distinguish between classes.

📈
Most classifiers don't predict a class directly — they output a probability score (e.g., 0.73 = 73% chance of being positive). You pick a threshold (e.g., 0.5) to convert it to a class. The ROC curve shows how Recall (TPR) and False Positive Rate change as you vary this threshold.

Interactive ROC Explorer

Move the threshold slider to see how the operating point shifts on the ROC curve. Watch how lowering the threshold catches more positives (higher recall) but also more false alarms.

What each axis means
Y-axis (TPR / Recall): Of all real positives, fraction correctly caught.
X-axis (FPR): Of all real negatives, fraction incorrectly flagged.
The diagonal is random guessing. A good model hugs the top-left corner.

AUC Interpretation
AUC = P(score_pos > score_neg)

AUC is the probability that a randomly chosen positive example gets a higher score than a randomly chosen negative example. AUC = 0.5 → random; AUC = 1.0 → perfect.

Interpreting AUC Values

AUC Range Interpretation Analogy
0.50 – 0.60 Failing — barely better than random Flipping a coin to diagnose disease
0.60 – 0.70 Poor — limited discriminating power A doctor making random hunches
0.70 – 0.80 Fair — acceptable in some contexts A junior doctor with limited experience
0.80 – 0.90 Good — suitable for most applications A seasoned specialist
0.90 – 1.00 Excellent — rare in real-world data A world-class diagnostician with lab results
⚠️
When AUC is misleading: With severely imbalanced data, AUC-ROC can look great even for mediocre models. In those cases, use AUC-PR (Precision-Recall curve) instead — it focuses only on the positive class and doesn't reward correct negative predictions.

📏
MAE
Mean Absolute Error

Average of the absolute differences between predictions and actual values. Every error is weighted equally.

Formula
MAE = (1/n) × Σ |yᵢ − ŷᵢ|
Plain English
Your model predicts house prices. On average, it's off by $12,500. That's the MAE — easy to interpret, same unit as the target.
Interpretable units Robust to outliers When all errors matter equally
📐
MSE & RMSE
Penalizes large errors more

Squares each error before averaging — large errors are punished disproportionately. RMSE takes the square root to restore original units.

Formulas
MSE = (1/n) × Σ (yᵢ − ŷᵢ)²
RMSE = √MSE
Plain English
In self-driving cars, a prediction error of 10m is not twice as bad as 5m — it could be fatal. MSE/RMSE ensures your model is punished heavily for big mistakes.
Sensitive to outliers When big errors are catastrophic RMSE in original units
📊
R² Score
Coefficient of Determination

Measures what proportion of variance in the target is explained by the model. R²=1 is perfect; R²=0 means the model is as good as predicting the mean; R²<0 means it's worse than the mean.

Formula
R² = 1 − (SS_res / SS_tot)
Plain English
"My model explains 87% of why house prices vary." The remaining 13% is unexplained — due to factors not in your data or inherent randomness.
Scale-independent Comparing models Can be misleading with non-linear
🔢
MAPE
Mean Absolute Percentage Error

Expresses errors as a percentage of the actual values. Great for communicating model accuracy in business settings where relative error matters more than absolute.

Formula
MAPE = (100/n) × Σ |yᵢ − ŷᵢ| / |yᵢ|
Plain English
"Our demand forecasting model is off by 8.3% on average." CEOs and stakeholders love percentages — they're context-free and comparable across different scales.
Business-friendly Fails when actual = 0 Forecasting, demand planning
Section 05

When to Use What

The hardest part of evaluation isn't computing metrics — it's choosing the right one. Here's your scenario-based decision guide.

🏥 Medical Diagnosis High Stakes

Missing a disease is catastrophic. A false alarm just triggers more tests — inconvenient but manageable. The cost of a False Negative is much higher than a False Positive.

→ Prioritize: Recall (Sensitivity)

📧 Spam Filter Asymmetric Cost

Losing an important email (false positive) is more annoying than receiving spam (false negative). You want the spam folder to be reliable — if it's flagged, it should really be spam.

→ Prioritize: Precision

💳 Fraud Detection Imbalanced

Fraud is rare (1 in 10,000 transactions). Accuracy is useless here. You need to catch fraud (recall) without blocking too many legit transactions (precision).

→ Prioritize: F1-Score, AUC-PR, MCC

🔍 Search Engine Ranking Ranking

Users care about what's at the top of the results. A relevant document buried on page 5 is nearly useless. Position matters enormously.

→ Prioritize: NDCG, MAP, MRR

🏠 House Price Prediction Regression

You want to know the average dollar error in same units as the target. Errors are roughly symmetric and large outlier errors aren't catastrophic.

→ Prioritize: MAE, RMSE

🌡️ Demand Forecasting Business KPI

Stakeholders want relative, not absolute, error. "We're off by $12,000" is less useful than "We're off by 4%" — especially when comparing products at different price scales.

→ Prioritize: MAPE, sMAPE

🐈 Image Classification Multi-class

Balanced classes, equal cost for all errors. The standard macro or weighted averages of classification metrics work well across all classes.

→ Prioritize: Accuracy, Macro F1

🔬 Drug Discovery Extreme Imbalance

Positive hits are extremely rare (1 in millions). AUC-ROC looks great but is meaningless. Focus entirely on the minority class performance.

→ Prioritize: MCC, AUC-PR, Recall @ k

🤖 LLM / Text Generation Generative

You need to evaluate generated text. Human judgment is best, but automated metrics can correlate with quality for quick iteration.

→ Prioritize: BLEU, ROUGE, BERTScore, Perplexity

📉 Stock Return Prediction Finance

Large prediction errors during high-volatility periods are especially harmful. The model should be penalized more for being wrong when it "should" be most accurate.

→ Prioritize: RMSE, R², Sharpe-aligned metrics

Quick Decision Framework

Step 1 — Problem Type
Predicting a category? → Classification metrics. Predicting a number? → Regression metrics.
Step 2 — Class Balance
Balanced? → Accuracy is fine. Imbalanced (1:10 or worse)? → Drop accuracy, use F1 / MCC / AUC-PR.
Step 3 — Error Costs
FP costly (false alarms) → Precision. FN costly (missed positives) → Recall. Both equal → F1.
Step 4 — Threshold Decisions
Choosing a single threshold? → Point metrics (P, R, F1). Want threshold-independent view? → AUC-ROC.
